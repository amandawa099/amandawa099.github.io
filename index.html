Flagship Case Study

Fixing a Costly, Invisible Pattern

The Problem

100+ newsletters a month, and a quiet flaw

For years, our department produced 100+ personalized client newsletters every month, each customized in content, branding, and tone to the individual salesperson it represented. It was a high-volume, detail-heavy process, and like most high-volume processes, it had a quiet flaw: two to three batches of newsletters each month were going out with errors serious enough to require reprints. Each reprint meant extra printing costs, extra shipping costs, and a client left waiting on materials they needed for their business. No one had flagged it as a fixable problem, it had just become the cost of doing business at scale.

What I Noticed

The errors weren't random

When I looked closer, they clustered around the same handoff point: the gap between "content finalized" and "batch shipped," where nothing was catching small mistakes before they became expensive ones. The process had a blind spot, not a skill problem, so nobody was doing anything wrong, the system just wasn't built to catch what it needed to catch.

What I Built

A checkpoint, and a way to see the whole pipeline

I designed and implemented a secondary QA checkpoint specifically at the handoff between printed and packed, a final review step that verified every newsletter had the correct content, correct count, and correct client details before it was sent out. I paired this with a color-coded tracking system I built in Excel so the whole team could see, at a glance, where every client stood in the production pipeline and when their information had last been updated. This closed the visibility gap that had let errors slip through unnoticed in the first place.

The Result
80–95%
Reduction in reprint errors

Reprint errors dropped from 2–3 per month to roughly one every six to twelve months. That translated directly into lower printing and shipping costs, fewer frustrated clients, and a process the whole team could trust.

What It Taught Me

The smallest intervention that closes the gap

The fix wasn't a big, dramatic overhaul, it was noticing exactly where a well-intentioned process was quietly failing, and building the smallest possible intervention that would close the gap. That's usually where the real leverage is: not in doing more work, but in finding the one point where a small, well-placed check prevents a much bigger cost down the line.

Predicting Failure Before It Happens

I became the go-to person for keeping our office's three printers running. Not because I had formal repair training, but because I paid close enough attention to notice the early signs of trouble before they turned into downtime: a feeder about to jam, a print roller wearing out, or a transfer belt about to give out. I kept a running log of every issue and fix, which meant we always had a record to back us up when it mattered. What started as "the person who can clear a jam fast" became "the person who kept the whole team from losing hours of production time."

Adapting Without Losing Ground

When our department switched design software from Microsoft Publisher to Affinity, I didn't just learn the new tool, I made sure we didn't lose any of the efficiencies I'd already built into our process. I found ways to consolidate documents and add new shortcuts along the way, so the transition ended up making our workflow faster than it had been before the switch.
